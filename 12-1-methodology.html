Troubleshooting Methodology

ğŸ“‹ What You'll Learn

Effective troubleshooting is both an art and a science. Having a systematic methodology prevents wasted time, missed issues, and incomplete resolutions. This guide covers proven approaches used by senior network and security engineers.

  • OSI model approach to troubleshooting
  • Divide and conquer methodology
  • Top-down vs bottom-up approaches
  • The importance of documentation
  • Change correlation analysis
  • Baseline comparison techniques
  • Escalation procedures

The Troubleshooting Mindset

Before diving into specific techniques, adopt the right mindset:

1

Stay Calm and Methodical

Panic leads to random changes and makes problems worse. Even during outages, a systematic approach is faster than guessing.

2

Question Everything

Don't assume. "It's always worked" doesn't mean the configuration is correct. Verify before moving on.

3

Document as You Go

Write down what you checked, what you found, and what you changed. This saves time if you need to escalate or rollback.

4

Change One Thing at a Time

If you change multiple things simultaneously, you won't know which one fixed (or broke) the issue.

The OSI Model Approach

Using the OSI model provides a systematic framework for isolating where a problem exists. Work from the bottom up or top down depending on the symptoms.

First Questions to Ask

When troubleshooting any network/security issue, these questions help narrow down the problem quickly:

Question Why It Matters
"Did this ever work?" If it never worked, likely a configuration issue. If it worked before, something changed.
"When did it stop working?" Correlate with change windows, maintenance, updates. Check change management logs.
"What changed?" Most problems are caused by changes. Even "nothing changed" often reveals something.
"Who is affected?" One user = likely client issue. One site = branch/WAN issue. Everyone = core/data center issue.
"Can you show me the error?" Seeing the actual error message prevents miscommunication. Screenshots are valuable.
"Have you tried from a different device/location?" Isolates whether issue is device-specific, network-specific, or universal.
âœ… Best Practice - The "What Changed?" Investigation

80% of incidents are caused by changes. When something breaks, systematically check:

  • Recent tickets: What changes were implemented today/yesterday?
  • Config audit logs: Who made firewall/switch changes recently?
  • Update history: Were any patches or signatures updated?
  • Monitoring alerts: Did any related alerts fire before the issue?
  • Infrastructure changes: New servers, VMs, network equipment?
  • External factors: ISP issues, third-party service outages?

âš ï¸ Common Troubleshooting Mistakes
  • Making changes without documenting: You'll forget what you changed and can't rollback properly
  • Assuming you know the answer: Always verify with data, not assumptions
  • Not checking the basics: Is it plugged in? Is the service running? Don't skip fundamentals.
  • Tunnel vision: If your theory isn't proving out, step back and consider other causes
  • Changing multiple things at once: You won't know what fixed it (or what broke it worse)
  • Not verifying after "fixing": Always confirm with the user/monitoring that it's actually resolved
  • Working in isolation: Ask colleagues if they've seen similar issues. Check knowledge base.

Common Question: "Walk me through how you would troubleshoot [scenario]."

Framework for answering:

  1. Gather info: "First, I'd understand the scope - who's affected, when it started, what changed"
  2. Develop theory: "Based on the symptoms, I'd hypothesize that..."
  3. Test systematically: "I'd use [specific tools] to verify at each layer..."
  4. Implement/verify: "Once I identify the cause, I'd implement the fix and verify with the user"
  5. Document: "Finally, I'd document the root cause and update our knowledge base"

Always mention specific tools you'd use (Wireshark, tcpdump, CLI commands) to show practical experience.
